# Confined Space Entry — Safety Procedure

A confined space is any area that is (a) large enough for a technician to
enter and perform work, (b) has limited or restricted means of entry/exit,
and (c) is not designed for continuous occupancy. Sewer lines,
crawlspaces, septic tanks, attics with single-point access, and equipment
vaults are the common ones in field service.

This is a non-optional procedure. If the job requires confined-space entry
and the conditions below cannot be met, do not enter — re-schedule with
the appropriate equipment.

## Before Entry

Confirm in writing (radio call to dispatch, photo of completed checklist):

1. **Atmospheric test.** Use a 4-gas meter to test for oxygen (must be
   19.5-23.5%), LEL (must be < 10%), CO (must be < 35 ppm), and H2S
   (must be < 10 ppm). Re-test every 60 minutes while inside.
2. **Ventilation.** If the atmospheric test fails or is borderline,
   ventilate with a blower for at least 5 minutes and re-test. Do not
   enter until atmosphere is verified safe.
3. **Standby attendant.** A second person remains outside the entry point
   for the duration of the work, in continuous radio or voice contact.
   The attendant's job is to extract the technician if anything goes
   wrong — they do not enter to help.
4. **Lockout / tagout.** Any energy source that could activate during the
   work (electrical, hydraulic, gas, water under pressure) must be locked
   out at the source with the technician's personal lock.
5. **Rescue plan.** Document the rescue path (how do we get the
   technician out if they go unconscious?). For most field jobs this is a
   harness + tripod + winch at the entry point.

## Entry Equipment

Required PPE for any confined-space entry:

- Full-body harness with retrieval D-ring (dorsal)
- Hard hat with chin strap
- Safety glasses
- Steel-toe boots
- 4-gas atmospheric meter (in-pocket, on)
- Hand torch (LED, not incandescent — incandescent in flammable
  atmospheres has caused fatalities)
- Two-way radio on the customer site's safety channel

## During Work

- Communicate with the attendant every 5 minutes ("status check") via
  radio. If three consecutive checks are missed, the attendant initiates
  rescue.
- Stop work immediately and exit if the 4-gas meter alarms, you feel
  dizzy or short of breath, or your sense of smell detects gas (H2S
  paralyzes the olfactory nerve quickly, so smell is unreliable — trust
  the meter).
- Do not bring uncertified tools into the space. Spark-resistant tools
  only if the space has any combustible-gas history.

## After Work

- Account for all tools and materials before exiting. A wrench left
  behind can be a missile in a pressurized restart.
- Sign the entry log on the way out with time of exit and final
  atmospheric reading.
- Notify dispatch that the entry is closed.

## Crawlspace Sewer Work — Common Hazards

Sewer lines in crawlspaces are a frequent service call. Specific risks:

- H2S accumulation in low spots even with a partial vent.
- Methane from anaerobic decomposition — undetectable by smell, lethal
  at high concentration.
- Hantavirus from rodent droppings — wear a P100 respirator if droppings
  are visible.
- Heat stroke in summer attics adjacent to crawlspaces.

If any of these are suspected and the 4-gas meter does not cover the
specific hazard, refer the call to a hazmat-certified contractor.
